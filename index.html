Quarter One

Foundation & Workplace Safety

OHS principles, ethical standards, transcription tools, hardware, and ergonomic workstation setup.

LESSON 01
Introduction to Medical Transcription
Medical Transcription (MT) is the process of converting voice-recorded medical reports dictated by physicians and healthcare professionals into accurate text format. It is a critical component of healthcare documentation.

Key Points:
  • MT bridges oral communication and written medical records
  • Transcriptionists work in hospitals, clinics, and BPO companies
  • NC II qualification prepares students for national certification (TESDA)
  • The field demands accuracy, confidentiality, speed, and medical knowledge
  • Output documents include: H&P reports, operative notes, discharge summaries, and consultation letters
LESSON 02
Occupational Health & Safety (OHS)
OHS in medical transcription protects transcriptionists from workplace hazards including ergonomic injuries, eye strain, and repetitive stress disorders.

OHS Principles:
  • Proper posture and ergonomic workstation setup
  • Regular breaks to prevent Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI)
  • Adequate lighting to reduce eye fatigue
  • Compliance with RA 11058 – Occupational Safety and Health Standards Act
  • Reporting of accidents, near-misses, and workplace hazards
  • Use of PPE where applicable
LESSON 03
Ethical Standards in Medical Transcription
Transcriptionists handle sensitive patient data and must follow strict ethical and legal standards.

Core Ethical Principles:
  • Confidentiality – Patient records must never be disclosed without authorization
  • Accuracy – Every word must be transcribed as dictated
  • Integrity – No fabrication, alteration, or omission of medical data
  • HIPAA – Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (US standard)
  • RA 10173 – Data Privacy Act of the Philippines
  • Code of Ethics – AHDI (Association for Healthcare Documentation Integrity)
LESSON 04
Transcription Tools & Hardware
Medical transcriptionists rely on specific tools and hardware to perform their work efficiently.

Essential Tools:
  • Foot Pedal – Controls audio playback hands-free (play, pause, rewind)
  • Headset/Headphones – For clear audio reception with noise cancellation
  • Computer/Laptop – Primary work device with sufficient processing speed
  • Transcription Software – Express Scribe, InScribe, oTranscribe, nVoq
  • Word Processor – MS Word for document creation and formatting
  • Medical Spellchecker – Specialized dictionaries for medical terms
LESSON 05
Workstation Setup & Ergonomics
A properly configured workstation minimizes physical strain and maximizes productivity during long transcription sessions.

Ergonomic Guidelines:
  • Monitor at eye level, approximately 50–60 cm from face
  • Chair adjusted so feet are flat on the floor, knees at 90°
  • Wrists straight, forearms parallel to the floor while typing
  • Keyboard and mouse within easy reach, no awkward stretching
  • Room temperature 20–24°C for comfort and concentration
  • Take a 5-minute break every hour — stretch, rest eyes (20-20-20 rule)
LESSON 06
Transcription Software & File Formats
Understanding audio and document formats is essential for handling various transcription assignments.

Common Audio Formats:
  • MP3, WAV, DSS, DS2, WMA, FLAC
Document Output Formats:
  • DOCX (MS Word), PDF, RTF, TXT
Key Software Features:
  • Variable speed playback (slow down dictation without pitch change)
  • Foot pedal integration and keyboard shortcuts
  • Auto-correct and medical word expanders
  • Timestamp and blank flagging tools

Human Anatomy & Body Systems

Structure and functions of the human body — essential foundation for accurate medical transcription.

LESSON 01
Overview of Human Body Organization
The human body is organized into levels: cells → tissues → organs → organ systems → organism.

Body Cavities:
  • Cranial – houses the brain
  • Thoracic – contains heart and lungs
  • Abdominal – contains digestive organs
  • Pelvic – contains reproductive and urinary organs
Anatomical Planes:
  • Sagittal (left/right), Coronal/Frontal (front/back), Transverse (top/bottom)
Directional Terms: anterior/posterior, superior/inferior, medial/lateral, proximal/distal
LESSON 02
Cardiovascular System
The cardiovascular system circulates blood throughout the body, delivering oxygen and nutrients while removing waste.

Key Structures:
  • Heart – 4 chambers: right/left atrium, right/left ventricle
  • Arteries – carry oxygenated blood away from the heart
  • Veins – carry deoxygenated blood back to the heart
  • Capillaries – microscopic vessels for gas/nutrient exchange
Transcription Terms: myocardial infarction (MI), arrhythmia, hypertension, atherosclerosis, ECG/EKG, angina pectoris, CHF
LESSON 03
Respiratory System
The respiratory system facilitates gas exchange — delivering oxygen to the blood and expelling carbon dioxide.

Key Structures (in order of airflow):
  • Nasal cavity → Pharynx → Larynx → Trachea
  • Bronchi → Bronchioles → Alveoli
  • Lungs (left: 2 lobes; right: 3 lobes)
  • Diaphragm – primary muscle of respiration
Transcription Terms: pneumonia, asthma, COPD, dyspnea, tachypnea, apnea, spirometry, TB, pleurisy
LESSON 04
Skeletal System
The skeletal system provides structural support, protects internal organs, produces blood cells, and stores minerals.

Key Facts:
  • 206 bones in the adult human body
  • Axial skeleton – skull, vertebral column (33 vertebrae), sternum, ribs (24)
  • Appendicular skeleton – limbs, shoulder girdle, pelvic girdle
  • Joints: fibrous, cartilaginous, synovial (most movable)
Transcription Terms: fracture, osteoporosis, osteoarthritis, scoliosis, lordosis, kyphosis, arthroscopy, ORIF
LESSON 05
Nervous & Endocrine Systems
The nervous system coordinates body activities through electrical signals; the endocrine system uses hormones for regulation.

Nervous System Divisions:
  • CNS: Brain (cerebrum, cerebellum, brainstem) and spinal cord
  • PNS: Cranial nerves (12 pairs), spinal nerves (31 pairs)
  • ANS: Sympathetic (fight-or-flight) and parasympathetic (rest-and-digest)
Endocrine Glands:
  • Pituitary (master gland), thyroid, parathyroid, adrenal, pancreas, gonads, pineal
Terms: stroke (CVA), TIA, epilepsy, neuropathy, diabetes mellitus, hypothyroidism, Cushing's syndrome
LESSON 06
Urinary & Reproductive Systems
The urinary system filters blood and excretes waste. The reproductive system enables human reproduction.

Urinary System:
  • Kidneys (2), ureters (2), urinary bladder, urethra
  • Nephron – functional unit of the kidney; filters blood to produce urine
  • GFR (Glomerular Filtration Rate) – key kidney function indicator
Reproductive System:
  • Male: testes, epididymis, vas deferens, prostate, seminal vesicles
  • Female: ovaries, fallopian tubes, uterus, cervix, vagina
Terms: UTI, cystitis, nephrolithiasis, urinalysis, gynecology, obstetrics, gravida, para, menstruation

Medical Terminology

Mastering Greek and Latin roots, prefixes, and suffixes to interpret and transcribe medical vocabulary with precision.

LESSON 01
Word Roots & Combining Forms
Medical terms are built from Greek and Latin word roots. A combining form adds a vowel (usually "o") to connect parts.

Common Roots:
  • cardi/o – heart  |  hepat/o – liver
  • nephr/o – kidney  |  oste/o – bone
  • derm/o – skin  |  gastr/o – stomach
  • pneum/o – lung/air  |  neur/o – nerve
  • hem/o – blood  |  arthr/o – joint
  • rhin/o – nose  |  ophthalm/o – eye
LESSON 02
Medical Prefixes
Prefixes modify the meaning of a word by appearing at its beginning.

Common Prefixes:
  • a-/an- – without (anemia, apnea)
  • brady- – slow (bradycardia)
  • tachy- – fast (tachycardia, tachypnea)
  • hyper- – excessive (hypertension, hyperglycemia)
  • hypo- – deficient (hypoglycemia, hypotension)
  • peri- – around (pericardium, periosteum)
  • poly- – many (polycystic, polydipsia)
  • sub- – below (subcutaneous, sublingual)
  • intra- – within (intravenous, intramuscular)
  • inter- – between (intercostal, intervertebral)
  • dys- – painful/difficult (dyspnea, dysuria)
  • macro-/micro- – large/small (macrocyte, microscopy)
LESSON 03
Medical Suffixes
Suffixes appear at the end of a word and often indicate a condition, procedure, or specialty.

Common Suffixes:
  • -itis – inflammation (appendicitis, tonsillitis)
  • -ectomy – surgical removal (appendectomy, cholecystectomy)
  • -oscopy – visual examination (colonoscopy, bronchoscopy)
  • -ology – study of (cardiology, neurology)
  • -plasty – surgical repair (rhinoplasty, angioplasty)
  • -algia – pain (neuralgia, myalgia)
  • -emia – blood condition (anemia, septicemia)
  • -oma – tumor/mass (carcinoma, melanoma)
  • -pathy – disease (neuropathy, cardiomyopathy)
  • -rrhea – flow/discharge (diarrhea, rhinorrhea)
  • -stenosis – narrowing (aortic stenosis)
  • -gram/-graphy – record/process of recording (cardiogram, radiography)
LESSON 04
Medical Abbreviations & Symbols
Medical abbreviations are widely used in dictations. Transcriptionists must know which to retain and which to spell out.

Frequently Dictated Abbreviations:
  • PRN – as needed  |  NPO – nothing by mouth
  • Dx – diagnosis  |  Rx – prescription/treatment
  • Sx – symptoms  |  Hx – history
  • CC – chief complaint  |  SOB – shortness of breath
  • BP – blood pressure  |  HR – heart rate
  • BID – twice daily  |  TID – three times daily  |  QID – four times daily
  • STAT – immediately  |  PO – by mouth (per os)
  • OD/OS/OU – right eye/left eye/both eyes
LESSON 05
Body System Terminology by Specialty
Each medical specialty has its own vocabulary that transcriptionists must master.

By Specialty:
  • Cardiology – MI, CHF, angina, arrhythmia, echocardiography, catheterization
  • Orthopedics – fracture, dislocation, arthroplasty, ORIF, bone density scan
  • Gastroenterology – GERD, colitis, endoscopy, hepatitis, colonoscopy
  • Neurology – CVA, TIA, seizure, dementia, EEG, lumbar puncture
  • OB-GYN – gravida, para, trimester, Cesarean section, pelvimetry, Pap smear
  • Pulmonology – COPD, asthma, TB, spirometry, bronchoscopy, PFT
  • Oncology – metastasis, chemotherapy, radiotherapy, staging, biopsy
LESSON 06
Pharmaceutical Terminology
Transcriptionists frequently encounter medication names and must distinguish between similar-sounding drug names (sound-alikes).

Drug Name Categories:
  • Generic name – official chemical name (e.g., amoxicillin)
  • Brand/Trade name – manufacturer's name (e.g., Amoxil) — always capitalized
Common Drug Classes:
  • Antibiotics: amoxicillin, azithromycin, ciprofloxacin, metronidazole
  • Antihypertensives: amlodipine, losartan, metoprolol, enalapril
  • Analgesics: paracetamol, ibuprofen, tramadol, morphine
  • Antidiabetics: metformin, insulin (Regular, NPH, Glargine), sitagliptin
  • Respiratory: salbutamol (Ventolin), budesonide, montelukast, theophylline

TOPIC 05
W5-5S of File Management in MT
Proper file management ensures transcribed documents are organized, retrievable, and secure.

The W5 of File Management:
  • What – type of document (H&P, Operative Note, Discharge Summary, Consult Letter)
  • Who – patient name, attending physician, facility
  • When – date and time of dictation and transcription (TAT tracking)
  • Where – storage location (local drive, cloud storage, EHR/EMR system)
  • Why – purpose, legal requirements, and retention policies
5S Workplace Principles:
  • Sort – remove unnecessary files
  • Set in Order – organize files logically
  • Shine – maintain clean, updated records
  • Standardize – consistent naming conventions and folder structures
  • Sustain – maintain habits through regular audits

TOPIC 07
Transcribing Audio Files in MT
The core practical skill — converting audio dictations into accurately formatted medical documents.

Step-by-Step Process:
  • 1. Load audio file into transcription software (e.g., Express Scribe)
  • 2. Set playback speed (70–80% for beginners; 100% for experienced)
  • 3. Use foot pedal to control play/pause/rewind hands-free
  • 4. Type as you listen; pause and rewind as needed
  • 5. Flag unclear dictations with blanks or comment markers
  • 6. Proofread and run medical spellcheck on the final document
  • 7. Format document per client/facility style guide
  • 8. Save in required format and submit within TAT
Quality Benchmarks: 60–75 WPM typing speed with ≥98% accuracy
TOPIC 08
Editing & Revising Text in MT
Quality assurance involves careful editing and revision to ensure accuracy and compliance with style guidelines.

Types of Editing:
  • Verbatim transcription – transcribe exactly as dictated, including false starts
  • Standard editing – correct obvious grammatical errors while retaining meaning
  • Intelligent verbatim – remove filler words (um, uh, you know) while keeping full meaning
Proofreading Checklist:
  • ✓ Correct patient demographics (name, DOB, MRN, date of service)
  • ✓ Accurate drug names, dosages, and frequencies
  • ✓ Consistent document formatting and section headings
  • ✓ No blank fields left unflagged for QA review
  • ✓ Proper punctuation, capitalization, and number formatting
  • ✓ Correct use of medical abbreviations per style guide

🧴 1. Integumentary System

Main Function: The integumentary system forms the external covering of the body. It protects internal organs from physical injury, pathogens, UV radiation, and dehydration. It also regulates body temperature, provides sensory information, and synthesizes Vitamin D.

Importance in MT: Skin conditions, wound documentation, burn classifications, and dermatology reports are frequently transcribed.

10 Key Organs / Structures

  • Epidermis – outermost layer; waterproof; contains keratinocytes and melanocytes
  • Dermis – middle layer; contains collagen, blood vessels, hair follicles, and nerve endings
  • Hypodermis (Subcutaneous layer) – deepest layer; fat and connective tissue for insulation
  • Hair follicles – produce hair; found in the dermis
  • Sebaceous glands – produce sebum (oil) to lubricate and waterproof skin
  • Sweat glands (Eccrine & Apocrine) – regulate temperature through perspiration
  • Nails – protective plates of keratin on fingertips and toes
  • Melanocytes – cells that produce melanin, the pigment that determines skin color
  • Langerhans cells – immune cells in the epidermis; first defense against pathogens
  • Merkel cells – sensory receptor cells in the epidermis; detect light touch

10 Conditions / Disorders

  • Acne vulgaris – inflammation of sebaceous glands and hair follicles
  • Psoriasis – chronic autoimmune condition causing red, scaly skin patches
  • Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis) – chronic inflammatory skin condition with itching and redness
  • Melanoma – malignant tumor of melanocytes; most dangerous skin cancer
  • Basal cell carcinoma – most common skin cancer; arises from basal layer of epidermis
  • Burns – classified as 1st (superficial), 2nd (partial thickness), 3rd (full thickness) degree
  • Cellulitis – bacterial infection of the dermis and subcutaneous tissue
  • Alopecia – hair loss, which may be localized or generalized
  • Urticaria (Hives) – raised, itchy welts caused by allergic reaction
  • Tinea (Ringworm/Athlete's Foot) – fungal infection of the skin

10 Treatments

  • Topical corticosteroids (hydrocortisone cream) – reduce inflammation in eczema and psoriasis
  • Retinoids (tretinoin, isotretinoin) – treat acne and promote cell turnover
  • Antifungal agents (clotrimazole, fluconazole) – treat tinea infections
  • Antibiotics (amoxicillin, cephalexin) – treat bacterial skin infections like cellulitis
  • Antihistamines (diphenhydramine, cetirizine) – relieve urticaria and itching
  • Surgical excision – removal of skin cancers (melanoma, BCC)
  • Cryotherapy – freezing with liquid nitrogen to destroy abnormal tissue
  • Phototherapy (UV light therapy) – used for psoriasis and eczema
  • Wound debridement and skin grafting – treatment for severe burns
  • Biologics (dupilumab) – targeted therapy for moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis

Medical Term Dissections

  • Dermatitis
    Prefix: n/a | Combining form: dermat/o (skin) | Combining vowel: /o/ | Suffix: -itis (inflammation) | Meaning: inflammation of the skin
  • Subcutaneous
    Prefix: sub- (under/below) | Combining form: cutane/o (skin) | Combining vowel: /o/ | Suffix: -ous (pertaining to) | Meaning: pertaining to below the skin
  • Melanoma
    Prefix: n/a | Combining form: melan/o (black/dark pigment) | Combining vowel: /o/ | Suffix: -oma (tumor/mass) | Meaning: tumor of melanin-producing cells
  • Onychomycosis
    Prefix: n/a | Combining form: onych/o (nail) + myc/o (fungus) | Combining vowel: /o/ | Suffix: -osis (abnormal condition) | Meaning: abnormal fungal condition of the nail
  • Erythroderma
    Prefix: n/a | Combining form: erythr/o (red) + derm/o (skin) | Combining vowel: /o/ | Suffix: -a (noun ending) | Meaning: diffuse redness of the skin
🦴 2. Skeletal System

Main Function: The skeletal system provides structural support and framework for the body, protects vital organs, enables movement in conjunction with muscles, produces blood cells (hematopoiesis), and stores minerals such as calcium and phosphorus.

Importance in MT: Orthopedic reports, fracture documentation, bone density studies, and joint procedures are highly common in transcription.

10 Key Organs / Structures

  • Skull (Cranium) – protects the brain; composed of 22 bones
  • Vertebral Column – 33 vertebrae (7 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral fused, 4 coccygeal fused)
  • Sternum – flat bone in the center of the chest; articulates with ribs
  • Ribs – 24 ribs (12 pairs); protect the thoracic organs
  • Femur – thigh bone; longest and strongest bone in the body
  • Tibia & Fibula – bones of the lower leg
  • Humerus – upper arm bone; articulates with shoulder and elbow
  • Radius & Ulna – bones of the forearm
  • Pelvis – bowl-shaped structure supporting abdominal organs; formed by ilium, ischium, and pubis
  • Cartilage – flexible connective tissue at joint surfaces and in structures like nose, ears, and trachea

10 Conditions / Disorders

  • Fracture – break in bone continuity; classified as simple, compound, comminuted, stress, etc.
  • Osteoporosis – decreased bone density increasing fracture risk; common in postmenopausal women
  • Osteoarthritis – degenerative joint disease; wearing down of articular cartilage
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis – autoimmune inflammation of synovial joints
  • Scoliosis – lateral curvature of the spine (S- or C-shaped)
  • Osteomyelitis – infection of bone, usually bacterial (S. aureus)
  • Bone cancer (Osteosarcoma) – malignant tumor of bone-forming cells
  • Gout – uric acid crystal deposition in joints causing severe pain
  • Dislocation – displacement of bone from its normal joint position
  • Herniated disc – protrusion of intervertebral disc nucleus compressing spinal nerves

10 Treatments

  • Cast or splint immobilization – used for fractures to allow bone healing
  • ORIF (Open Reduction Internal Fixation) – surgical alignment and fixation of fractured bones
  • Total Joint Replacement (THR/TKR) – replacement of damaged hip or knee joint
  • Calcium and Vitamin D supplementation – prevention and treatment of osteoporosis
  • Bisphosphonates (alendronate, risedronate) – slow bone loss in osteoporosis
  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) – reduce pain and inflammation in arthritis and gout
  • Corticosteroid injections – reduce joint inflammation in rheumatoid arthritis
  • Spinal fusion – surgical joining of vertebrae to treat severe scoliosis or herniated disc
  • Colchicine / Allopurinol – manage acute gout attacks and reduce uric acid levels
  • Physical therapy and rehabilitation – restore mobility and strength post-fracture or surgery

Medical Term Dissections

  • Osteoporosis
    Prefix: n/a | Combining form: oste/o (bone) | Combining vowel: /o/ | Suffix: -porosis (porous condition) | Meaning: porous/weakened condition of the bone
  • Arthralgia
    Prefix: n/a | Combining form: arthr/o (joint) | Combining vowel: /o/ | Suffix: -algia (pain) | Meaning: pain in a joint
  • Osteomyelitis
    Prefix: n/a | Combining form: oste/o (bone) + myel/o (marrow) | Combining vowel: /o/ | Suffix: -itis (inflammation) | Meaning: inflammation of bone and bone marrow
  • Chondromalacia
    Prefix: n/a | Combining form: chondr/o (cartilage) | Combining vowel: /o/ | Suffix: -malacia (softening) | Meaning: softening of cartilage
  • Spondylosis
    Prefix: n/a | Combining form: spondyl/o (vertebra/spine) | Combining vowel: /o/ | Suffix: -osis (abnormal condition) | Meaning: degenerative condition of the vertebrae (spinal degeneration)
🦾 3. Muscular System

Main Function: The muscular system enables movement of the body and internal organs, maintains posture, produces body heat through metabolic activity, and pumps blood (cardiac muscle). Muscles work in antagonistic pairs — when one contracts (agonist), the opposing muscle relaxes (antagonist).

Importance in MT: Musculoskeletal reports, physical therapy notes, and sports medicine documentation frequently involve muscle terminology.

10 Key Organs / Structures

  • Skeletal muscle – voluntary, striated; attached to bones via tendons (e.g., biceps, quadriceps)
  • Smooth muscle – involuntary, non-striated; found in walls of hollow organs
  • Cardiac muscle – involuntary, striated; found only in the heart
  • Tendons – fibrous connective tissue connecting muscle to bone
  • Trapezius – upper back and neck; moves and stabilizes the shoulder blade
  • Deltoid – shoulder; arm abduction
  • Rectus Abdominis – anterior abdominal wall; trunk flexion
  • Quadriceps Femoris – anterior thigh (4 heads); knee extension
  • Hamstrings – posterior thigh (3 muscles); knee flexion and hip extension
  • Gastrocnemius – calf; plantarflexion of the foot

10 Conditions / Disorders

  • Myalgia – muscle pain, often from overuse or infection
  • Myositis – inflammation of skeletal muscle tissue
  • Muscular Dystrophy – progressive genetic muscle weakness (Duchenne, Becker types)
  • Fibromyalgia – widespread musculoskeletal pain with fatigue and sleep disturbances
  • Rhabdomyolysis – rapid skeletal muscle breakdown releasing myoglobin; can cause renal failure
  • Strain – overstretching or tearing of muscle or tendon fibers
  • Tendinitis – inflammation of a tendon, commonly at the shoulder, elbow, or knee
  • Myopathy – disease of muscle tissue affecting structure or function
  • Contracture – permanent shortening of muscle or tendon causing reduced range of motion
  • Compartment Syndrome – dangerous increase in pressure within a muscle compartment compromising blood flow

10 Treatments

  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) – relieve muscle pain and inflammation
  • Muscle relaxants (cyclobenzaprine, baclofen) – reduce muscle spasm
  • RICE protocol (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) – first aid for strains and sprains
  • Physical therapy – strengthen and rehabilitate injured muscles
  • Corticosteroid injections – reduce inflammation in tendinitis
  • Surgical fasciotomy – emergency release of compartment syndrome pressure
  • IV fluid resuscitation – flushes myoglobin from kidneys in rhabdomyolysis
  • Occupational therapy – helps patients with muscular dystrophy maintain function

🧠 4. Nervous System

Main Function: The nervous system detects and interprets sensory information, coordinates voluntary and involuntary body responses, regulates internal organ function, and enables higher cognitive functions including memory, thought, and emotion.

Importance in MT: Neurology and neurosurgery reports involve complex terminology for brain conditions, spinal injuries, and neurological procedures.

10 Key Organs / Structures

  • Brain – control center; divided into cerebrum, cerebellum, and brainstem
  • Spinal cord – relay pathway between brain and body; housed in vertebral column
  • Cerebrum – largest brain region; controls voluntary movement, sensation, speech, memory
  • Cerebellum – coordinates balance, posture, and fine motor movements
  • Brainstem – controls basic life functions: breathing, heart rate, consciousness (midbrain, pons, medulla)
  • Cranial nerves – 12 pairs; transmit sensory and motor signals to the head and neck
  • Spinal nerves – 31 pairs; transmit signals between spinal cord and body
  • Neurons – basic functional cells of the nervous system; transmit electrical signals
  • Meninges – three protective membranes surrounding brain and spinal cord (dura mater, arachnoid, pia mater)
  • Synapse – junction between neurons where neurotransmitters are released

10 Conditions / Disorders

  • Stroke (CVA) – cerebrovascular accident; sudden loss of blood supply to brain tissue
  • Transient Ischemic Attack (TIA) – temporary stroke-like symptoms; "mini-stroke"
  • Epilepsy – recurrent seizures due to abnormal electrical brain activity
  • Parkinson's Disease – progressive loss of dopamine-producing neurons; tremors, rigidity
  • Alzheimer's Disease – progressive dementia with memory loss and cognitive decline
  • Multiple Sclerosis (MS) – autoimmune demyelination of CNS nerve fibers
  • Meningitis – inflammation of the meninges; bacterial or viral
  • Guillain-Barré Syndrome – autoimmune attack on peripheral nerves; ascending paralysis
  • Neuropathy – nerve damage causing pain, numbness, or weakness
  • Concussion – mild traumatic brain injury from impact; temporary loss of function

10 Treatments

  • tPA (tissue plasminogen activator) – clot-dissolving drug for ischemic stroke (within 3–4.5 hours)
  • Antiepileptic drugs (phenytoin, valproate, levetiracetam) – control seizures in epilepsy
  • Levodopa/Carbidopa – dopamine replacement therapy for Parkinson's Disease
  • Cholinesterase inhibitors (donepezil, rivastigmine) – slow cognitive decline in Alzheimer's
  • Disease-modifying therapies (interferons, natalizumab) – reduce MS relapse frequency
  • Antibiotics (cefotaxime, penicillin) – treat bacterial meningitis
  • IVIG (Intravenous Immunoglobulin) – used in Guillain-Barré Syndrome
  • Deep Brain Stimulation (DBS) – surgical implant for advanced Parkinson's Disease
  • Craniotomy / Neurosurgery – remove tumors, hematomas, or relieve spinal cord compression
  • Rehabilitation therapy (physical, occupational, speech) – post-stroke recovery

🔬 5. Endocrine System

Main Function: The endocrine system regulates body functions through chemical messengers called hormones, which are secreted directly into the bloodstream by ductless glands. It controls metabolism, growth, reproduction, mood, and response to stress.

Importance in MT: Endocrinology dictations frequently include hormone levels, gland disorders, and metabolic conditions like diabetes mellitus and thyroid disease.

10 Key Organs / Structures

  • Pituitary gland – "master gland"; secretes hormones that regulate other glands (GH, TSH, ACTH, FSH, LH, ADH, oxytocin)
  • Thyroid gland – butterfly-shaped; produces T3, T4 (metabolism regulation) and calcitonin (calcium regulation)
  • Parathyroid glands – 4 small glands behind thyroid; secrete PTH to raise blood calcium
  • Adrenal glands – sit atop kidneys; cortex produces cortisol, aldosterone; medulla produces epinephrine and norepinephrine
  • Pancreas (Islets of Langerhans) – alpha cells secrete glucagon; beta cells secrete insulin
  • Pineal gland – produces melatonin; regulates sleep-wake cycles (circadian rhythm)
  • Thymus – produces thymosin for T-cell maturation; most active in childhood
  • Gonads (Ovaries/Testes) – produce estrogen/progesterone (ovaries) and testosterone (testes)
  • Hypothalamus – brain region that links nervous and endocrine systems; releases releasing/inhibiting hormones
  • Kidneys – endocrine role: produce erythropoietin (EPO) and renin; activate Vitamin D

10 Conditions / Disorders

  • Diabetes Mellitus Type 1 – autoimmune destruction of beta cells; absolute insulin deficiency
  • Diabetes Mellitus Type 2 – insulin resistance and relative insulin deficiency; most common type
  • Hypothyroidism – underactive thyroid; low T3/T4; fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance
  • Hyperthyroidism (Graves' Disease) – overactive thyroid; weight loss, tachycardia, exophthalmos
  • Cushing's Syndrome – excess cortisol; moon face, buffalo hump, hypertension, hyperglycemia
  • Addison's Disease – adrenal insufficiency; low cortisol; fatigue, hypotension, hyperpigmentation
  • Acromegaly – excess GH after growth plate closure; enlarged hands, feet, jaw
  • Diabetes Insipidus – ADH deficiency; excessive urination and thirst
  • Hyperparathyroidism – excess PTH; elevated blood calcium; kidney stones, bone loss
  • Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) – hormonal imbalance in women; irregular periods, excess androgens, cysts

10 Treatments

  • Metformin – first-line oral antidiabetic for Type 2 DM
  • Levothyroxine (Synthroid) – thyroid hormone replacement for hypothyroidism
  • Propylthiouracil (PTU) / Methimazole – reduce thyroid hormone production in hyperthyroidism
  • Radioactive iodine (I-131) therapy – destroys overactive thyroid tissue
  • Hydrocortisone/Fludrocortisone – hormone replacement in Addison's Disease
  • Ketoconazole / surgical adrenalectomy – reduce cortisol in Cushing's Syndrome
  • Octreotide / transsphenoidal surgery – treat acromegaly
  • Desmopressin (DDAVP) – synthetic ADH for diabetes insipidus
  • Oral contraceptives / metformin – manage PCOS symptoms

🫀 6. Cardiovascular System

Main Function: The cardiovascular system circulates blood throughout the body via the heart and blood vessels, delivering oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and immune cells to tissues while removing carbon dioxide and metabolic waste products.

Importance in MT: Cardiology is one of the most transcription-heavy specialties. Reports include ECG interpretations, catheterization procedures, cardiac surgery notes, and echo reports.

10 Key Organs / Structures

  • Heart – muscular pump with 4 chambers (RA, LA, RV, LV) and 4 valves (mitral, tricuspid, aortic, pulmonary)
  • Aorta – largest artery; carries oxygenated blood from left ventricle to systemic circulation
  • Coronary arteries – supply blood to the heart muscle itself (LAD, RCA, LCx)
  • Pulmonary arteries – carry deoxygenated blood from right ventricle to the lungs
  • Veins (Superior/Inferior Vena Cava) – return deoxygenated blood from body to right atrium
  • Capillaries – microscopic vessels where oxygen/nutrient exchange with tissues occurs
  • Arteries – thick-walled vessels carrying blood away from the heart
  • Veins – thin-walled vessels with valves returning blood to the heart
  • Sinoatrial (SA) Node – heart's natural pacemaker; generates electrical impulse
  • Atrioventricular (AV) Node – delays impulse between atria and ventricles; coordinates contraction

10 Conditions / Disorders

  • Hypertension – persistently elevated blood pressure (>140/90 mmHg)
  • Myocardial Infarction (MI) – heart attack; necrosis of heart muscle due to blocked coronary artery
  • Congestive Heart Failure (CHF) – heart unable to pump sufficient blood; fluid buildup in lungs and body
  • Atrial Fibrillation (AFib) – irregular, rapid atrial electrical activity; risk of stroke from clot formation
  • Coronary Artery Disease (CAD) – atherosclerotic plaque buildup in coronary arteries; angina
  • Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) – blood clot in deep vein, usually leg; risk of pulmonary embolism
  • Aortic Aneurysm – abnormal dilation of the aortic wall; risk of rupture
  • Cardiac Tamponade – fluid in pericardial sac compressing the heart
  • Valvular Heart Disease – stenosis or regurgitation of heart valves (mitral, aortic)
  • Peripheral Artery Disease (PAD) – atherosclerosis of peripheral arteries; limb ischemia

10 Treatments

  • Antihypertensives (amlodipine, losartan, metoprolol, enalapril) – control blood pressure
  • Anticoagulants (warfarin, heparin, apixaban) – prevent clotting in AFib and DVT
  • Thrombolytics (streptokinase, tPA) – dissolve clots in acute MI and PE
  • Statins (atorvastatin, simvastatin) – lower LDL cholesterol; slow atherosclerosis
  • Aspirin / Clopidogrel – antiplatelet therapy to prevent MI recurrence
  • Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI) / Angioplasty with stenting – open blocked coronary artery
  • CABG (Coronary Artery Bypass Grafting) – bypass blocked coronary arteries using grafts
  • Pacemaker / ICD insertion – regulate heart rate and prevent sudden cardiac death
  • Diuretics (furosemide) – remove excess fluid in CHF
  • Pericardiocentesis – drain pericardial fluid in cardiac tamponade

🫧 7. Lymphatic System

Main Function: The lymphatic system maintains fluid balance by returning excess interstitial fluid to the bloodstream, supports immune defense through lymphocyte activation and antibody production, and absorbs dietary fats (as chyle) from the small intestine via lacteals.

Importance in MT: Lymphadenopathy is one of the most common findings in clinical dictations. Oncology and immunology reports heavily involve this system.

10 Key Organs / Structures

  • Lymph nodes – bean-shaped filters of lymph; site of lymphocyte activation; clustered in neck, axilla, and groin
  • Spleen – largest lymphatic organ; filters blood; destroys old RBCs; stores platelets
  • Thymus – site of T-lymphocyte maturation; located in mediastinum; most active in childhood
  • Bone marrow – produces all blood cells including B and T lymphocytes
  • Tonsils – palatine, pharyngeal (adenoids), lingual; guard against inhaled/ingested pathogens
  • Thoracic duct – largest lymph vessel; drains lower body and left upper body into left subclavian vein
  • Right lymphatic duct – drains right arm and right side of head/neck
  • Lymph capillaries – blind-ended vessels absorbing interstitial fluid in tissues
  • Peyer's patches – lymphoid tissue in ileum; defense against gut pathogens
  • Lacteals – specialized lymph capillaries in intestinal villi that absorb dietary fats (chyle)

10 Conditions / Disorders

  • Lymphadenopathy – enlargement of lymph nodes; common in infection, inflammation, and malignancy
  • Hodgkin's Lymphoma – cancer of lymphatic tissue characterized by Reed-Sternberg cells
  • Non-Hodgkin's Lymphoma – heterogeneous group of lymphoid malignancies; more common than Hodgkin's
  • Lymphedema – chronic swelling due to lymph fluid accumulation; primary or secondary (post-surgical)
  • Splenomegaly – enlarged spleen; associated with infections, liver disease, blood disorders
  • Tonsillitis – inflammation of tonsils; bacterial (Strep) or viral; may require tonsillectomy
  • Infectious Mononucleosis – EBV infection; fatigue, sore throat, lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly
  • HIV/AIDS – destroys CD4+ T-helper cells; progressive immune system failure
  • Lymphangitis – inflammation of lymph vessels; often appears as red streaks on skin near wound
  • Castleman Disease – rare benign tumor-like growth of lymph node tissue

10 Treatments

  • Antibiotics (amoxicillin, penicillin) – treat bacterial tonsillitis and lymphangitis
  • CHOP chemotherapy (cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, vincristine, prednisone) – treat lymphomas
  • Radiation therapy – used in Hodgkin's lymphoma treatment
  • Antiretrovirals (ARVs) – control HIV; ART (Antiretroviral Therapy) prevents progression to AIDS
  • Tonsillectomy & Adenoidectomy – surgical removal for recurrent tonsillitis
  • Compression bandaging / lymphatic massage – manage lymphedema
  • Splenectomy – surgical removal of ruptured or diseased spleen
  • IVIG – used in immune deficiency disorders to supplement antibodies
  • Bone marrow transplant / Stem cell transplant – used in lymphoma and leukemia
  • Monoclonal antibodies (rituximab) – targeted B-cell therapy in non-Hodgkin's lymphoma

🫁 8. Respiratory System

Main Function: The respiratory system facilitates gas exchange — delivering oxygen (O₂) from the air to the bloodstream and expelling carbon dioxide (CO₂) from the body. It also filters, warms, and humidifies inhaled air and aids in phonation (speech).

Importance in MT: Pulmonology, critical care, and emergency medicine reports extensively involve respiratory terminology, ABG results, and ventilator settings.

10 Key Organs / Structures

  • Nasal cavity – filters, warms, and humidifies incoming air; lined with cilia and mucus
  • Pharynx – throat; common passageway for air and food; divided into nasopharynx, oropharynx, laryngopharynx
  • Larynx – "voice box"; contains vocal cords (true and false); epiglottis prevents aspiration
  • Trachea – "windpipe"; ~12 cm long; reinforced by C-shaped cartilage rings
  • Bronchi (Primary) – two main branches of trachea entering each lung
  • Bronchioles – smaller airways within lung tissue; no cartilage; smooth muscle allows bronchoconstriction/dilation
  • Alveoli – microscopic air sacs where gas exchange occurs; coated with surfactant to prevent collapse
  • Lungs – right (3 lobes: upper, middle, lower) and left (2 lobes: upper, lower); surrounded by pleura
  • Pleura – double-layered membrane surrounding each lung; visceral and parietal layers; pleural fluid reduces friction
  • Diaphragm – primary muscle of respiration; contracts to increase thoracic volume and draw air in

10 Conditions / Disorders

  • Pneumonia – infection and inflammation of lung parenchyma; bacterial, viral, or fungal
  • Asthma – chronic inflammatory airway disease; episodic bronchospasm; wheezing and dyspnea
  • COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease) – emphysema and chronic bronchitis; irreversible airflow obstruction
  • Tuberculosis (TB) – Mycobacterium tuberculosis infection of lungs; productive cough, hemoptysis, night sweats
  • Pulmonary Embolism (PE) – blood clot in pulmonary artery; sudden dyspnea, chest pain, hypoxia
  • Pleural Effusion – fluid accumulation in pleural space; compresses lung tissue
  • Pneumothorax – air in pleural space; collapsed lung; may be spontaneous or traumatic
  • Lung Cancer – most common: adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, small cell carcinoma
  • Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS) – severe acute lung injury; widespread alveolar damage
  • Sleep Apnea – recurrent upper airway obstruction during sleep; hypoxia; treated with CPAP

10 Treatments

  • Bronchodilators (salbutamol/albuterol) – relax airway smooth muscle; relieve bronchospasm in asthma
  • Inhaled corticosteroids (budesonide, fluticasone) – reduce airway inflammation in asthma and COPD
  • Antibiotics (amoxicillin, azithromycin, levofloxacin) – treat bacterial pneumonia
  • Anti-TB drugs (RIPE: rifampicin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide, ethambutol) – 6-month regimen for TB
  • Anticoagulants (heparin, enoxaparin, warfarin) – treat and prevent pulmonary embolism
  • Thoracentesis – needle aspiration of pleural effusion
  • Chest tube insertion – drain pneumothorax or large pleural effusion
  • Mechanical ventilation / Intubation – support breathing in respiratory failure (ARDS, severe pneumonia)
  • CPAP / BiPAP – non-invasive positive pressure ventilation for sleep apnea and COPD
  • Oxygen therapy (O₂ via nasal cannula or mask) – correct hypoxemia in most respiratory conditions

🫃 9. Digestive System

Main Function: The digestive system breaks down ingested food into absorbable nutrients (carbohydrates, proteins, fats, vitamins, minerals, and water) that are transported to body cells for energy, growth, and repair. It also eliminates indigestible waste as feces.

Importance in MT: Gastroenterology and surgery reports frequently involve GI endoscopy findings, surgical procedure notes, and hepatology reports.

10 Key Organs / Structures

  • Mouth (Oral Cavity) – mechanical digestion via teeth; chemical digestion via salivary amylase
  • Esophagus – muscular tube; transports food via peristalsis; LES (lower esophageal sphincter) prevents reflux
  • Stomach – stores and churns food; HCl and pepsin begin protein digestion; produces chyme
  • Small intestine – ~6–7 m; major digestion and absorption site; three segments: duodenum, jejunum, ileum
  • Large intestine (Colon) – ~1.5 m; absorbs water and electrolytes; forms feces; houses gut microbiome
  • Liver – produces bile; detoxifies blood; metabolizes nutrients; stores glycogen and vitamins
  • Gallbladder – stores and concentrates bile; releases it into duodenum when fat is present
  • Pancreas – exocrine: digestive enzymes (amylase, lipase, proteases); endocrine: insulin, glucagon
  • Rectum – stores feces before defecation; stretch receptors trigger defecation reflex
  • Anus – external opening; internal (involuntary) and external (voluntary) sphincters control defecation

10 Conditions / Disorders

  • GERD – gastroesophageal reflux disease; chronic acid reflux due to LES dysfunction
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD) – ulcers in stomach or duodenum; H. pylori or NSAID-related
  • Appendicitis – inflammation of appendix; McBurney's point tenderness; treated by appendectomy
  • Cholecystitis – gallbladder inflammation; usually from gallstones (cholelithiasis)
  • Hepatitis (A, B, C) – viral liver inflammation; B and C can become chronic
  • Cirrhosis – irreversible liver scarring; portal hypertension, ascites, esophageal varices
  • Crohn's Disease – IBD; transmural inflammation anywhere in GI tract; skip lesions
  • Ulcerative Colitis – IBD; mucosal inflammation limited to colon and rectum
  • Colorectal Cancer – most arise from adenomatous polyps; CEA tumor marker; screened via colonoscopy
  • Pancreatitis – inflammation of the pancreas; acute (gallstones/alcohol) or chronic

  • H. pylori eradication (triple therapy: amoxicillin + clarithromycin + PPI) – treat H. pylori-related ulcers
  • Appendectomy (laparoscopic or open) – surgical removal of inflamed appendix
  • Laparoscopic cholecystectomy – gold standard for cholecystitis and gallstones
  • Antiviral therapy (sofosbuvir, ledipasvir) – treat Hepatitis C; curative in most cases
  • Lactulose / Rifaximin – manage hepatic encephalopathy in cirrhosis
  • Aminosalicylates (mesalazine) + Corticosteroids – treat IBD flares
  • Biologics (infliximab, adalimumab) – treat moderate-to-severe Crohn's and UC
  • Colonoscopy with polypectomy – remove colorectal polyps before they become malignant
  • IV fluid + NPO + analgesics – initial management of acute pancreatitis

💧 10. Urinary System

Main Function: The urinary system filters metabolic waste products from the blood, regulates fluid and electrolyte balance (sodium, potassium, chloride), controls blood pH, maintains blood pressure through renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS), and excretes waste as urine.

Importance in MT: Nephrology and urology reports include dialysis notes, kidney function tests (BUN, creatinine, GFR), and urological surgical procedures.

10 Key Organs / Structures

  • Kidneys (×2) – bean-shaped organs in the retroperitoneal space; filter ~180 L of blood daily
  • Nephron – functional unit of the kidney; ~1 million per kidney; filters blood to produce urine
  • Glomerulus – network of capillaries in Bowman's capsule; site of filtration
  • Renal tubules – proximal convoluted tubule, loop of Henle, distal convoluted tubule; reabsorb and secrete solutes
  • Collecting duct – final concentration of urine; responds to ADH (antidiuretic hormone)
  • Renal pelvis – funnel-shaped cavity collecting urine from calyces before it enters the ureter
  • Ureters (×2) – muscular tubes transporting urine from kidneys to bladder via peristalsis (~25–30 cm)
  • Urinary bladder – hollow muscular organ storing urine (capacity ~400–600 mL); detrusor muscle contracts during micturition
  • Urethra – tube from bladder to outside; female (~4 cm), male (~20 cm, also carries semen)
  • Adrenal glands – sit atop kidneys; secrete aldosterone affecting fluid and electrolyte balance

10 Conditions / Disorders

  • Urinary Tract Infection (UTI) – bacterial infection of bladder (cystitis) or kidneys (pyelonephritis)
  • Nephrolithiasis (Kidney Stones) – calcium oxalate, uric acid, or struvite crystals in urinary tract
  • Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) – progressive loss of kidney function; staged by GFR (Stage 1–5)
  • Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) – sudden decline in kidney function; elevated BUN and creatinine
  • Nephrotic Syndrome – protein loss in urine; edema, hypoalbuminemia, hyperlipidemia
  • Nephritic Syndrome – hematuria, hypertension, oliguria; glomerular inflammation
  • Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD) – genetic disorder; multiple cysts replace kidney tissue
  • Bladder Cancer – most common: transitional cell carcinoma; painless hematuria
  • Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH) – enlarged prostate obstructing urinary flow in males
  • Urinary Incontinence – involuntary leakage of urine; stress, urge, or overflow types

10 Treatments

  • Antibiotics (trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, nitrofurantoin, ciprofloxacin) – treat UTI and pyelonephritis
  • IV hydration + analgesics + alpha-blockers (tamsulosin) – facilitate passage of kidney stones
  • Lithotripsy (ESWL) – non-invasive shockwave fragmentation of kidney stones
  • Dialysis (Hemodialysis or Peritoneal) – renal replacement therapy in CKD Stage 5 (ESRD)
  • Kidney transplantation – definitive treatment for end-stage renal disease
  • ACE inhibitors (enalapril, lisinopril) – slow CKD progression and reduce proteinuria
  • Diuretics (furosemide, hydrochlorothiazide) – manage fluid overload in CKD and nephrotic syndrome
  • TURP (Transurethral Resection of the Prostate) – surgical treatment for BPH
  • 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (finasteride) / Alpha-blockers (tamsulosin) – medical management of BPH
  • Cystoscopy with biopsy / intravesical BCG therapy – diagnose and treat bladder cancer

  • Ureteroscopy
    Prefix: n/a | Combining form: ureter/o (ureter) | Combining vowel: /o/ | Suffix: -scopy (visual examination) | Meaning: visual examination of the ureter
🛡️ 11. Immune System

Main Function: The immune system defends the body against pathogens (bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites), abnormal cells (cancer), and foreign substances. It involves physical barriers, innate (non-specific) immunity for immediate defense, and adaptive (specific) immunity for targeted, memory-based defense.

Importance in MT: Immunology and infectious disease reports, allergy documentation, transplant rejection notes, and autoimmune disorder dictations are common in transcription.

10 Key Organs / Structures

  • White blood cells (Leukocytes) – main cellular components; neutrophils, lymphocytes, monocytes, eosinophils, basophils
  • B Lymphocytes (B cells) – produce antibodies (immunoglobulins); responsible for humoral immunity
  • T Lymphocytes (T cells) – destroy infected or cancerous cells; responsible for cellular immunity; CD4+ (helper) and CD8+ (cytotoxic)
  • Natural Killer (NK) cells – innate lymphocytes that destroy virus-infected and tumor cells
  • Macrophages – phagocytic cells that engulf pathogens; present antigens to T cells
  • Dendritic cells – antigen-presenting cells that activate T cells; link innate and adaptive immunity
  • Antibodies (Immunoglobulins) – proteins produced by B cells; neutralize antigens (IgG, IgA, IgM, IgE, IgD)
  • Complement system – cascade of proteins that lyse pathogens and enhance phagocytosis
  • Cytokines – signaling proteins (interleukins, interferons, TNF) that regulate immune responses
  • Skin and mucous membranes – first line of physical defense against pathogen entry

10 Conditions / Disorders

  • HIV/AIDS – Human Immunodeficiency Virus; destroys CD4+ T cells; leads to immunodeficiency
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA) – autoimmune attack on synovial joints; erosive joint destruction
  • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) – autoimmune multi-organ disease; butterfly rash, nephritis, arthritis
  • Anaphylaxis – severe systemic allergic reaction; IgE-mediated; life-threatening hypotension and airway compromise
  • Allergic Rhinitis – IgE-mediated nasal inflammation from airborne allergens
  • Primary Immunodeficiency – congenital absence of immune components (e.g., SCID, DiGeorge syndrome)
  • Sepsis – life-threatening systemic immune response to infection; SIRS criteria
  • Graft-versus-Host Disease (GVHD) – donor immune cells attack recipient tissues after transplant
  • Multiple Myeloma – malignant proliferation of plasma cells; excess abnormal antibody production
  • Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus – autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells

10 Treatments

  • Antiretroviral therapy (ART) – suppress HIV viral load; prevent AIDS progression
  • DMARDs (methotrexate, hydroxychloroquine) – slow disease progression in RA and SLE
  • Biologics (adalimumab, etanercept, rituximab) – target specific immune mediators in autoimmune diseases
  • Corticosteroids (prednisone, methylprednisolone) – broad immunosuppression for acute flares
  • Epinephrine (adrenaline) auto-injector (EpiPen) – first-line emergency treatment for anaphylaxis
  • Antihistamines (diphenhydramine, cetirizine) – relieve mild allergic symptoms
  • IVIG (Intravenous Immunoglobulin) – replace antibodies in primary immunodeficiency
  • Vaccines – stimulate adaptive immunity; prevent infectious diseases
  • Immunosuppressants (tacrolimus, cyclosporine, mycophenolate) – prevent transplant rejection and manage GVHD
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics + IV fluids + vasopressors – initial management of sepsis (Sepsis Bundles)

Medical Term Dissections

  • Immunodeficiency
    Prefix: n/a | Combining form: immun/o (immune/protection) | Combining vowel: /o/ | Suffix: -deficiency (lack of) | Meaning: lack of adequate immune protection
  • Anaphylaxis
    Prefix: ana- (against/up) | Combining form: phylax/o (protection/guard) | Combining vowel: /o/ | Suffix: -is (condition) | Meaning: condition of extreme immune over-reaction to a substance
  • Autoimmune
    Prefix: auto- (self) | Combining form: immun/o (immune/protection) | Combining vowel: /o/ | Suffix: -e (adjective ending) | Meaning: immune system directed against the body's own tissues
  • Phagocytosis
    Prefix: n/a | Combining form: phag/o (to eat/engulf) + cyt/o (cell) | Combining vowel: /o/ | Suffix: -osis (process/condition) | Meaning: process of cells engulfing and destroying foreign particles
  • Immunoglobulin
    Prefix: n/a | Combining form: immun/o (immune) + globul/o (protein) | Combining vowel: /o/ | Suffix: -in (substance) | Meaning: immune protein substance (antibody)
🌸 12. Reproductive System

Main Function: The reproductive system is responsible for the production of offspring. It produces gametes (sperm and ova), provides the environment for fertilization and fetal development (female), and secretes sex hormones (estrogen, progesterone, testosterone) that regulate secondary sexual characteristics and reproductive cycles.

Importance in MT: OB-GYN, urology, and fertility clinic reports are highly specialized dictation types involving detailed anatomical and procedural terminology.

10 Key Organs / Structures

  • Ovaries (×2) – female gonads; produce ova (eggs) and hormones (estrogen, progesterone)
  • Fallopian tubes (Uterine tubes) – transport ova from ovaries to uterus; site of fertilization
  • Uterus – hollow muscular organ for fetal development; three layers: endometrium, myometrium, perimetrium
  • Cervix – lower narrow portion of uterus; opens during labor; Pap smear site
  • Vagina – muscular canal from cervix to external genitalia; birth canal
  • Testes (×2) – male gonads; produce spermatozoa and testosterone; located in the scrotum
  • Epididymis – coiled tube on the posterior testes; site of sperm maturation and storage
  • Vas deferens (Ductus deferens) – muscular tube carrying sperm from epididymis to ejaculatory duct
  • Prostate gland – walnut-sized gland; produces prostatic fluid contributing to semen; surrounds urethra
  • Seminal vesicles – produce fructose-rich fluid nourishing sperm; contribute ~60% of semen volume

10 Conditions / Disorders

  • Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) – hormonal imbalance; irregular menstruation, excess androgens, ovarian cysts
  • Endometriosis – endometrial tissue outside the uterus; dysmenorrhea, infertility
  • Uterine Fibroids (Leiomyoma) – benign smooth muscle tumors of the uterus; heavy menstrual bleeding
  • Cervical Cancer – most associated with HPV infection; detected by Pap smear
  • Ovarian Cancer – often asymptomatic until late stage; CA-125 tumor marker
  • Ectopic Pregnancy – implantation outside the uterus (usually fallopian tube); medical emergency
  • Prostate Cancer – most common cancer in men; PSA (prostate-specific antigen) screening
  • Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH) – non-cancerous prostate enlargement; obstructive urinary symptoms
  • Erectile Dysfunction (ED) – inability to achieve or maintain erection adequate for sexual activity
  • Infertility – inability to conceive after 12 months of unprotected intercourse; male or female factor

10 Treatments

  • Oral contraceptive pills (OCPs) – regulate menstrual cycle; treat PCOS, endometriosis, and dysmenorrhea
  • Metformin + Clomiphene citrate – stimulate ovulation in PCOS
  • Laparoscopic excision/ablation – surgical treatment of endometriosis
  • Hysterectomy – surgical removal of uterus; treatment for fibroids, endometrial cancer, or refractory uterine conditions
  • HPV vaccine (Gardasil, Cervarix) + Pap smear screening – prevent and detect cervical cancer
  • Methotrexate (IM injection) / Salpingectomy – medical and surgical treatment of ectopic pregnancy
  • Radical prostatectomy / Radiation therapy – treat prostate cancer
  • Finasteride + Tamsulosin – medical management of BPH
  • PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil/Viagra, tadalafil/Cialis) – treat erectile dysfunction
  • ART (Assisted Reproductive Technology): IVF, IUI – treat infertility in couples
